Pedagogy spine

Seven design principles, built into the content itself

These aren’t a preface a teacher has to remember — they’re structural. Each shapes how every objective is authored and rendered, which is what lets the same curriculum serve a preliterate newcomer and a Level 3 student in the same room.

P1

Oral-first

Listening and Speaking lead every unit; Reading and Writing follow once meaning is anchored. Matches how newcomers — especially SLIFE — actually acquire language.

P2

Low text, high visual

Photos, icons, gesture, and realia carry meaning first. Text arrives scaffolded, never as the only route in.

P3

Language-agnostic scaffolds

Supports work regardless of home language: visuals, cognate awareness, audio, and an L1 “bridge” slot a student, peer, or aide fills in any language.

P4

Foundational-literacy strand

A parallel track — alphabet, letter–sound, print concepts, handwriting — runs through every unit for students without home-language literacy.

P5

Delivery-agnostic

One objective renders as a print packet, a projector routine, a device task, or small-group cards. The teacher’s setup decides, not the curriculum.

P6

Personalized by proficiency

Every objective exists at Levels 1, 2, and 3, per domain. The engine serves the right variant and moves a student up as evidence accrues.

P7

Assets, not deficits

Home languages and lived knowledge are resources. Translanguaging is invited, and content treats a newcomer’s prior learning as a starting strength.

Scope & Sequence · ~24 weeks

Twelve units, three phases — survival to academic on-ramp

The arc moves from “get through the school day” to narrating and explaining — the doorway into content-area language. Units are modular: a pull-out group can take the whole sequence; a push-in teacher can pull a single objective into a mainstream lesson. Two strands — foundational literacy and oracy-first — run through all twelve.

“Introduce yourself” — what personalization looks like

One Speaking objective, served three ways. The student output is in this reading face; the colored blanks are scaffolded frames. This is the difference between a worksheet and a platform.

ScaffoldSentence frames + a world map. Fills three slots, produces the string aloud from the model.

From curriculum to product

Why the content model is the moat

Everything above is one data model. A unit is a set of Objective Cards; each card carries its three leveled variants, its scaffolds, and its render targets. The personalization engine places a student on a WIDA level per domain and serves the matching variant. The same card compiles to a printable PDF, a projector slide, or an interactive task — so print-first districts and 1:1 districts buy the same product. And every completed check emits evidence that rolls up into the growth reporting administrators need to justify the purchase.

Author the curriculum as cards, and the software is largely a rendering-and-routing problem on top of good content — which is exactly the order you chose: curriculum first.

Assumptions. ~24-week arc at roughly two weeks per unit, sized for a designated ELD block (~45 min/day); modular for push-in, pull-out, newcomer-class, and blended use. Grade clusters 6–8 and 9–12. Proficiency band 1–3 of WIDA’s six levels. Foundational-literacy and oracy-first strands run through all units.

Standards. Independent alignment to the WIDA English Language Development Standards Framework (2020 Edition) — Standards 1 (Social & Instructional) and 2 (Language Arts), the four language domains, Key Language Uses, and Can-Do descriptors.
